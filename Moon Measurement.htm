Moon Measurement

In March 2025, a lunar eclipse occurred, visible from most of America. I had just finished building my homemade refracting telescope at that time.
Doing the logical thing, I hosted a spontaneous mini star lunar party in the yard of the university housing.
About 10-20 people came and took a whole lot of pictures using my telescope, some having better
quality than others. A thought passed my mind, what if, using these high-quality pictures, I compare them to other pictures taken from, say, Mexico,
and use that to measure the distance from the Earth to the Moon!


This was the image taken by my friend (Leon)'s phone that morning, at totality, 3:00 a.m.

And this was the image taken by a Seattle-based astrophotographer by the name of Roger Barnett I had found through Facebook, at 2:58 a.m.


I then used the visible stars HIP 56388 and HIP 56376 on the right side of the image to align the image, since the parallax of background stars compared
to the moon would be very, very small. This next image is me using Canvas to layer it on top of one another

Finally, using a software I used to edit fantasy maps I larped with, paint.net, I counted the parallax angel in pixels, it came to about 330.13 pixels, which can
be seen with the apparent size of the moon above.

I hadn't mentioned the eyepiece I used, but it was a 30mm Ploessl, resulting in a 20 times magnification with my 600mm refractor, with 50 degrees AFOV,
meaning the TFOV would be 50/20 = 2.5 degrees

I took this value and counted the diameter of theentire FOV of my picture, which was 1727 pixels. Meaning the degree/pixel value is 2.5/1727 = 0.001447596989

Now, we use trigonometry and the distance (through the Earth) between Toronto and Seattle. It is roughly 1592.03160852 km halfway there, using the Google Earth feature
to measure the surface distance between my observing spot and the middle of the city, and then employing a bit of trigonometry before dividing it by two.
We just need to plug these values into the formula: distance to the moon = distance of cities/tan(0.5*parallax angle)

Of course, we still need to calculate the actual parallax angle which is: 0.001447596989*330.13 = 0.477895193979 degrees

Now the distance = 1592.03160852 km/tan(0.5* 0.477895193979) = 381,741.3 km!! Which was only a 4.35% error since the actual distance at the time was 399,100 km

While the distance is actually the distance from the moon to the chord that exists between Toronto and Seattle, but, considering the large numbers we are
dealing with, it shouldn't be too far off from the actual value, whether or not the cities are farther or closer.
